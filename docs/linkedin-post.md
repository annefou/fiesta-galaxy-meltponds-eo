We keep telling researchers: *publish your workflows so others can reuse them.* But
a pipeline sitting in a registry — however FAIR — rarely crosses into a new field
on its own. You only grasp what a tool can do for **you** when someone puts it in
**your** context.

Humans are bad at abstracting away from our own discipline. AI, honestly, often is
too. So the missing step isn't *more publishing* — it's **replication across
disciplines**: taking a pipeline built for one field and actually running it, with
new inputs, in another. That's what triggers the "oh — I could use that for…"
moment.

A small proof, from OSCARS–FIESTA 🛰️🔬

We took the Galaxy Training Network's *"Introduction to Image Analysis"* tutorial —
the one that counts stained **cell nuclei** under a microscope — and, without
changing a single tool, pointed it at **Sentinel-2 satellite imagery** to count
**supraglacial melt ponds** on the Greenland ice sheet.

Same threshold → label → count → measure chain. Result: ~90 melt ponds, ~1.5 km² of
meltwater — and when we benchmarked it against an **independently published lake
map**, the off-the-shelf workflow recovered **~88% of the mapped lake area**. The
only things that changed were the *index* (a water index instead of a stain) and
the *threshold*.

That's the real lesson. What lets a workflow travel isn't the code alone — it's the
precise **metadata about its input**: the "contract" that says what the image must
be (one index per pixel, target objects as peaks of that index, a separating
threshold, a known pixel size → area). Make that contract explicit and FAIR, and
the cell-counting pipeline counts melt ponds.

And because reuse should be **creditable**, we didn't just run it — we replicated
the training for ourselves and recorded that reuse as a **CiTO nanopublication
chain**: atomic, machine-readable provenance that credits the tutorial, the tools
and the method we built on.

Everything is open and reproducible — the Galaxy workflow, a public history you can
import, a Snakemake + notebook pipeline, the validation against the published
lakes, and (transparency all the way down) **the very script that generated the
figure above**.

The takeaway for open science: don't just publish pipelines and hope. **Replicate
them into other disciplines.** That's how latent reuse becomes real reuse — and how
new ideas get triggered.
